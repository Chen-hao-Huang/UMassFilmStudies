Decasia

Directed by Bill Morrison
Screening Date: March 8, 2017
7:30PM, 137 Isenberg SOM
Run Time: 67 Minutes


Black and white stock footage, much of it scratched or blistered, illustrates a Michael Gordon symphony. A whirling dervish, couples laughing, a soldier trying to take advantage of a flower vendor, a camel caravan moving across the horizon, a single plane and then others, paratroopers in the sky, a mining disaster, a pugilist, nuns and children at a school - some images last a few seconds, others for a minute or more. The scratches, blisters, and bygone look of the footage suggest time's passage. Only the dervish, who begins and ends the film, is intact.

Directed by visionary experimental filmmaker Bill Morrison, Decasia is a meditation on old, decaying silent films. With no outside acceleration of the film’s decaying process, Morrison presents a vivid, visually-rich ode to days of filmmaking past.


Synopsis and contextualization of the film provided by critic Jonathan Jones, detailing the backstory of the progress and Morrison’s personal history as a filmmaker. The history of the silent film era and its decline is also discussed throughout the article, giving more clarity to a somewhat puzzling film.

Kimi Takesue is an award-winning independent filmmaker exploring several genres, including documentary, narrative, and experimental. Her films often explore the complicated nature of cross-cultural interaction.
